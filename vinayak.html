Algorithms & Nature — Summary

Concise notes on how nature reflects algorithmic ideas, plus complexity, data structures, and design techniques – formatted as Q&A style cards.

1. Problems in Nature (Iteration, Recursion, Backtracking)

Nature mirrors computational patterns:

  • Iteration: Seasonal cycles repeatedly execute like loops.
  • Recursion: Self-similar structures (branching, segments) repeat at different scales.
  • Backtracking: Animals explore paths, retreat from dead-ends, and try alternatives.

2. Space & Time Efficiency

Space efficiency is extra memory used; time efficiency is runtime. Together they determine scalability.

  • O(1), O(log n), O(n), O(n log n)
  • O(n²), O(2ⁿ), O(n!)

3. Design Principles

  • BFS vs DFS as brave vs cautious exploration.
  • Prefix/suffix reasoning drives pattern matching (KMP, Boyer–Moore).
  • Balancing operations maintain tree performance (AVL, red-black).

4. Hierarchical Data & Trees

  • Binary/AVL/Red-black/2–3 trees for ordered data.
  • Heaps for priority scheduling.
  • Tries for fast prefix and dictionary operations.

5. Array Query Algorithms

  • Fenwick tree for prefix sums with low memory.
  • Segment tree for flexible range queries/updates.
  • Precomputation tables for O(1) queries over small domains.

6. Trees vs Graphs & Traversals

Trees: connected, acyclic, rooted, n−1 edges.

Graphs: general vertex/edge structure, may have cycles or be disconnected.

Traversals: tree traversals (pre/in/post) vs BFS/DFS on graphs for connectivity and shortest paths.

7. Sorting & Searching

  • Bubble/Insertion/Selection: simple, good for teaching and small n.
  • Merge sort: stable O(n log n).
  • Quick sort: average O(n log n) with good pivots.
  • Linear vs binary search depending on order; graph searches for network data.

8. Graph Algorithms

Spanning trees (MST) minimize connection cost; shortest paths minimize travel time/cost in networks, important for routing, logistics, and navigation.

9. Design Techniques Overview

  • Backtracking for exhaustive search with pruning.
  • Divide & conquer for efficient decomposition.
  • Shortest path algorithms for weighted graphs.
  • Graph traversals as core exploration tools.
