Course Reflections — Design & Analysis of Algorithms

Rewritten, highlighted, and structured for presentation or GitHub pages

1. What kinds of problems do we observe in nature? (iteration, recursion, backtracking)

Nature mirrors computational paradigms through repeating cycles, self-similar patterns, and exploratory decision-making.

Iteration: Seasonal cycles — spring, summer, monsoon, winter — act as iterative loops that reoccur until external conditions alter the pattern.

Recursion: Structural repetition seen in organisms such as millipedes demonstrates smaller units reflecting the whole.

Backtracking: Observed when animals like rabbits traverse complex burrow systems and retreat to prior junctions upon reaching dead-ends.

2. What is space and time efficiency? Why do they matter? Explain the different classes of problems and orders of growth.

Space efficiency = extra memory an algorithm consumes.
Time efficiency = execution duration required to finish computations.

Both attributes determine scalability, cost, and practical feasibility for large-scale systems.

3. Takeaway from different design principles (Chapter 2)

Design principles act as heuristics that shape algorithm choice and structure. They make complex choices predictable and maintainable.

Traversal analogies: Brave exploration vs cautious exploration map to BFS and DFS respectively.

Pattern matching: Prefixes and suffixes are central to efficient substring search (KMP, Boyer–Moore).

Balancing: Rotations and rebalancing in AVL and red-black trees preserve operation efficiency across inserts/deletes.

4. Hierarchical data — trees and optimisations (BST, AVL, 2-3, Red-Black, Heap, Trie)

Trees model hierarchical relationships effectively. Key points:

  • Binary Tree: Base structure, easy to traverse.
  • Binary Search Tree (BST): Fast search when balanced; risks skewing.
  • AVL Tree: Strictly balanced via rotations — predictable heights.
  • 2–3 Trees: Multiple keys per node to maintain low height.
  • Red–Black Tree: Easier to implement balancing rules with good performance.
  • Heap: Priority-based structure; good for scheduling and selection.
  • Trie: Efficient prefix operations — autocomplete, dictionary tasks.

5. The need for array query algorithms and their implications

Range queries and point updates are fundamental in many applications:

  • E-commerce filters (price ranges)
  • Financial analysis (stock ranges over time)
  • Fenwick Tree (BIT): compact, bitwise trick for fast prefix sums
  • Lookup tables: precompute results to answer queries instantly

6. Differentiate between trees and graphs and their traversals; applications

Trees: connected, acyclic, rooted structures with exactly n–1 edges.

Graphs: general vertex-edge models that can be cyclic or disconnected; no inherent root.

Traversals:

  • Tree: Pre-order, In-order, Post-order — used in expression evaluation, BST ops.
  • Graph: DFS (deep exploration, backtracking) and BFS (level-wise, shortest paths in unweighted graphs).

7. Sorting and searching algorithms — techniques and connections to real world

Sorting arranges data; searching retrieves elements. Highlights:

  • Bubble/Selection/Insertion: simple, educational but inefficient for large n.
  • Merge Sort: stable divide-and-conquer with O(n log n) guarantees.
  • Quick Sort: average-case O(n log n), in-place, pivot selection matters.
  • Searching: Linear (unsorted) vs Binary (sorted); graph searches for relational data.

Applications: database indexing, product ranking, file search, information retrieval.

8. Importance of graph algorithms — spanning trees and shortest paths

Spanning trees: connect all nodes with minimal total weight — useful in network design (utilities, telecoms).

Shortest path: minimises travel cost/time — vital for routing, logistics, and navigation systems.

9. Algorithm design techniques studied

Key techniques:

  • Backtracking: exhaustive search with pruning (N-Queens).
  • Divide and Conquer: break and merge strategies (Merge/Quick Sort).
  • Shortest Path: Dijkstra, Floyd–Warshall for weighted graphs.
  • Graph Traversals: BFS and DFS as foundational exploration methods.
